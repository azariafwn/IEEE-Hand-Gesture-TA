\section{Proposed Method}
The proposed system focuses on the computational pipeline of sequence-based hand gesture recognition executed locally on a Raspberry Pi 5. This section details the software architecture, data preprocessing, model design, and evaluation metrics used to validate the system.

\subsection{Software Pipeline Architecture}

The proposed system focuses entirely on the computational pipeline of sequence-based hand gesture recognition, executed locally on a Raspberry Pi 5. The core software architecture is designed to process video streams in real-time, extracting spatial features and classifying temporal sequences without relying on external cloud processing. 

As illustrated in Fig. \ref{fig:software_pipeline}, the data processing flow consists of four sequential stages:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Video Acquisition:} The system captures continuous video frames from a standard webcam at 30 frames per second (FPS).
    \item \textbf{Spatial Feature Extraction:} Each raw frame is processed using the MediaPipe Hands framework to extract the spatial coordinates of 21 3D hand landmarks \cite{han2022mediapipe}.
    \item \textbf{Temporal Sequence Buffering:} The extracted spatial coordinates are flattened and stored in a rolling buffer. A complete sequence consists of 30 consecutive frames, effectively capturing the temporal dynamics of the gesture over a one-second window.
    \item \textbf{Inference and State Management:} Once the buffer reaches its capacity, the temporal sequence is fed into the quantized TFLite LSTM model. The resulting predicted gesture class is then processed by a State Machine to validate the command, handle timeouts, and trigger the corresponding smart home logic output.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Gesture Vocabulary and Data Preprocessing}

The proposed system is designed to facilitate intuitive multi-device control within a smart home environment. As illustrated in Fig. \ref{fig:device_topology}, the Raspberry Pi 5 serves as the central processing unit, managing interaction flows for four distinct target appliances: a smart light (Device 1), an electric fan (Device 2), a smart light (Device 3), and a smart plug / smart power socket (Device 4). This multi-device configuration necessitates a robust interaction logic to prevent unintended activations.

A comprehensive dataset was constructed involving 8 different subjects to capture natural human movement variability and develop a high-generalization model. Data acquisition was performed under diverse environmental conditions, including variations in lighting (bright, ambient, and low-light) and non-uniform backgrounds to prevent model overfitting. The distance between the subjects' hands and the camera was varied within a range of 40 cm to 60 cm, with the hand positioned dynamically across different areas of the frame. This proses resulted in a total of 1,998 samples, with approximately 200 samples per gesture class, providing a balanced distribution for training and evaluation.

The system utilizes a 10-class physical gesture dataset mapped to 6 logical control commands to provide initial-state invariance. As detailed in Table \ref{tab:gesture_classes}, the vocabulary includes Action Gestures for state toggling and Selection Gestures for device targeting. Users can transition fluidly between commands without returning to a rigid, predefined neutral pose by providing two physical variations for each Selection Gesture, originating from either a closed fist (\texttt{close\_to\_x}) or an open palm (\texttt{open\_to\_x}).

Following sequence capture, spatial feature extraction is performed using the MediaPipe Hands framework. As illustrated in Fig. \ref{fig:mediapipe_landmarks}, MediaPipe identifies and extracts the spatial coordinates of 21 3D hand landmarks for each individual video frame \cite{han2022mediapipe}.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig/landmark-mediapipe.png}
    \caption{Topology of the 21 3D hand landmarks extracted by MediaPipe.}
    \label{fig:mediapipe_landmarks}
\end{figure}

The raw Cartesian coordinates $(x, y, z)$ of each landmark are geometrically normalized relative to the wrist point (Landmark 0) to ensure the model remains robust and invariant to the user's distance from the camera and hand size variations. Once extracted and normalized, the spatial coordinates from the 21 landmarks are flattened into a single one-dimensional vector per frame. Specifically, the 21 points multiplied by 3 coordinates yield a 63-dimensional spatial feature vector, denoted as $v_t \in \mathbb{R}^{63}$, which represents the complete hand posture at time step $t$.

These frame-level vectors are accumulated sequentially over time to capture the temporal dynamics of the movement. In this study, a complete gesture sequence is defined by a temporal window of $T = 30$ consecutive frames. Consequently, the final preprocessed data fed into the Deep Learning model is structured as a two-dimensional numerical tensor, $X$, representing the sequence of spatial vectors:

\begin{equation}
X = 
\begin{bmatrix}
v_1 \\
v_2 \\
\vdots \\
v_{m}
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{x}_{11}, \mathbf{y}_{11}, \mathbf{z}_{11} & \cdots & \mathbf{x}_{1n}, \mathbf{y}_{1n}, \mathbf{z}_{1n} \\
\mathbf{x}_{21}, \mathbf{y}_{21}, \mathbf{z}_{21} & \cdots & \mathbf{x}_{2n}, \mathbf{y}_{2n}, \mathbf{z}_{2n} \\
\vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
\mathbf{x}_{m1}, \mathbf{y}_{m1}, \mathbf{z}_{m1} & \cdots & \mathbf{x}_{mn}, \mathbf{y}_{mn}, \mathbf{z}_{mn}
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

The resulting matrix $X$ represents a complete gesture sequence where $m$ denotes the total number of temporal steps and $n$ represents the total number of extracted hand landmarks. In this study, the sequence length is defined as $m = 30$ frames, while the spatial features consist of $n = 21$ landmarks. Since each landmark is represented by three-dimensional bold Cartesian coordinates $(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{z})$, each row in the matrix $X$ contains $3n$ numerical values. 

Consequently, the final input tensor has a shape of $(m, 3n)$, which in this configuration corresponds to a $(30, 63)$ dimensionality. This matrix structure is essential for the subsequent Stacked LSTM architecture to learn the hierarchical temporal patterns of the dynamic gestures, utilizing double-precision floating-point formatting (\texttt{float64}) to maintain spatial precision across all $m$ time steps.


\subsection{Deep Learning Architecture}

This study employs a Stacked Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network to effectively learn the temporal dependencies inherent in dynamic hand movements. LSTM networks are explicitly designed to overcome the vanishing gradient problem in standard recurrent networks, making them highly effective for processing the sequentially buffered $(30, 63)$ spatial feature tensors \cite{sengupta2020review}. The model builds a hierarchical representation of the temporal data through a stacked architecture, where deeper layers capture higher-level abstractions of the gesture dynamics \cite{yu2019review}. The complete layer-by-layer configuration of the proposed network is detailed in Table \ref{tab:model_architecture}.

As shown in the architectural summary, the core feature extraction engine consists of three stacked LSTM layers with 64, 128, and 64 units, respectively. The first two LSTM layers are configured to return the full sequence, allowing the subsequent layer to analyze the temporal progression comprehensively. The final LSTM layer extracts the most salient temporal features and returns a flattened 64 dimensional vector. 

Dropout layers with a rate of 0.5 are strategically inserted after the first and second LSTM layers to mitigate the risk of overfitting during training, which is a common challenge in deep sequence models. This stochastic regularization technique randomly drops 50\% of the neural connections during each training epoch. The network is thus forced to learn robust, generalized representations rather than memorizing the training data \cite{labach2019survey}.

Following the recurrent stages, the network utilizes a deep classifier composed of two dense layers with 64 and 32 units, employing the Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) activation function for non-linear transformations. ReLU is chosen for its ability to overcome the vanishing gradient problem in deep networks and its computational efficiency compared to sigmoidal functions \cite{dubey2022activation}. Finally, the output layer consists of 10 dense units with a \texttt{softmax} activation function, which provides a normalized probability distribution suitable for multi-class classification tasks. This configuration computes the probability distribution across the 10 specific physical gesture classes (as defined in Table \ref{tab:gesture_classes}).



\subsection{Edge Optimization and Deployment}

The trained 32-bit floating-point (FP32) Keras model undergoes post-training quantization to achieve real-time inference on the resource-constrained Raspberry Pi 5. Specifically, the model is converted into the TensorFlow Lite (TFLite) format utilizing 8-bit integer (INT8) quantization, which is a framework designed to enable deep learning inference on ultra-low-power embedded systems \cite{david2021tflite}. This optimization strategy fundamentally reduces the memory footprint and accelerates inference speed by substituting computationally expensive floating-point arithmetic with efficient integer operations optimized for ARM-based embedded processors \cite{orasan2022quantization}.

The entire software stack is containerized using Docker during the deployment phase to ensure dependency isolation and cross-platform reproducibility. While containerization introduces a negligible computational overhead, it guarantees stable, continuous operation within the edge computing environment without the risk of system library conflicts \cite{potdar2020docker}.

Finally, to translate raw model predictions into robust smart home commands, the deployment incorporates a custom State Machine logic layer. This component mitigates false positives by enforcing a strict chronological sequence: a Selection Gesture (e.g., pointing to \texttt{DEVICE\_1}) must be followed by an Action Gesture (e.g., \texttt{ACTION\_ON}). The integration of a Finite State Machine (FSM) provides a deterministic framework to manage complex gesture transitions and reduce erroneous command triggers by validating the logical flow of user interactions \cite{noparlik2023hand}. Furthermore, a temporal timeout mechanism is implemented; if a valid Action Gesture is not detected within a 3-second window following a device selection, the system autonomously resets to an \texttt{IDLE} state. This logic discards pending operations, thereby ensuring interaction safety and preventing unintended appliance activation.



\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

A comprehensive evaluation is conducted using standard machine learning metrics derived from a confusion matrix to rigorously assess the performance of the proposed dynamic gesture classification model. Relying solely on overall accuracy can be misleading in multi-class classification tasks; therefore, Precision, Recall, and the F1-Score are employed to analyze the model's predictive reliability across all 10 physical gesture classes.

The foundation of these metrics relies on four outcomes: True Positives ($TP$), True Negatives ($TN$), False Positives ($FP$), and False Negatives ($FN$). Based on these components, the evaluation metrics are mathematically defined as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Accuracy:} The ratio of correctly predicted observations to the total observations. It provides a general overview of system performance \cite{grandini2020metrics}.
    \begin{equation}
        \text{Accuracy} = \frac{TP + TN}{TP + TN + FP + FN}
    \end{equation}

    \item \textbf{Precision:} The ratio of correctly predicted positive observations to the total predicted positives. In the context of smart home control, high precision ensures that when the system detects a specific gesture, it is highly likely to be correct, thereby minimizing accidental appliance triggers \cite{grandini2020metrics}.
    \begin{equation}
        \text{Precision} = \frac{TP}{TP + FP}
    \end{equation}

    \item \textbf{Recall (Sensitivity):} The ratio of correctly predicted positive observations to all observations in actual class. High recall indicates that the system rarely misses a user's intended gesture \cite{grandini2020metrics}.
    \begin{equation}
        \text{Recall} = \frac{TP}{TP + FN}
    \end{equation}

    \item \textbf{F1-Score:} The harmonic mean of Precision and Recall. This metric is crucial for determining the optimal balance between exactness (Precision) and completeness (Recall), especially when dealing with subtle variations in human hand movements \cite{grandini2020metrics}.
    \begin{equation}
        \text{F1-Score} = 2 \times \frac{\text{Precision} \times \text{Recall}}{\text{Precision} + \text{Recall}}
    \end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

These metrics are calculated for each individual gesture class to identify any specific classification bottlenecks, ensuring the system meets the rigorous reliability standards required for seamless ambient interaction.
